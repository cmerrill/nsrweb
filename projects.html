RFID Reader/Cloner

For my final project in 6.115 (the MIT microcontroller lab class) I made an integrated RFID reader and spoofer.

This photo shows the system's PCB antenna. It also had pads exposed for additional circuitry used to turn the antenna into a RF-powered, reprogrammable RFID spoofing device. The spoofer could be used in its antenna mode to read MIT ID cards, and could be programmed to emulate any MIT ID card that it had read.

Unfortunately time constraints limited my ability to fully implement the spoofing feature, but all other parts of the system function at full capacity. For more information (including schematics) you can read my design proposal.

Mass-Produced Yo-Yo

2.008 is an MIT mechanical engineering class that teaches the techniques required for design and manufacturing on a large scale. The core of the class is a course-long project to design, manufacture, and assemble a set of 50 Yo-Yos.

I was in a group of six people collaborating on a design. Our yo-yo had super-heavy steel washers that fit in to the body and gave it a high moment of inertia. The result of this was an incredibly stable yo-yo (due to gyroscopic forces) that had a friendly heft.

Vincent Lee and I worked together to design the injection-molded body for the yo-yo. We designed the shape (based of a revolution of a parabolic curve), machined the positive and negative mold, and optimized the injection parameters to produce a consistent result.

Capacitive-Discharge Spot Welder

In the fall of my seior year I took 6.131, Professor Steve Leeb's power electronics lab. The course covered the basics of converting one form of energy into another — including power converters (DC/DC, AC/DC/AC, AC/AC), actuators (solenoids, motors, lights), and feedback control systems.

The class has a free-form final project. For mine, I wanted to build something that I felt would be a useful tool. Given that jumping off point, I decided to build a capacitive-discharge spot welder.
